The Midnight Cartographers

Every night after the city clocks blinked 12:00, three cats met on the roof
of the old observatory: Juniper, who could read the wind; Marmalade, who had
never met a locked window he respected; and Professor Whisk, whose left paw
always pointed toward adventure.

The humans thought the observatory had been abandoned for years. They did not
know that beneath its copper dome, the cats kept a glowing map of every warm
chimney, moonlit alley, hidden fish stall, and secret garden in the city.

One stormy evening, a new star appeared where no star belonged. It pulsed
silver-blue and dropped a beam of light onto a tiny courtyard no cat had ever
mapped. Juniper narrowed her green eyes. Marmalade packed three sardines for
the road. Professor Whisk adjusted his velvet bow tie and declared that any
unmapped courtyard was a personal insult.

They crossed rain-slick rooftops, leapt over laundry lines, and rode a tram
for seven blocks by pretending to be a very serious parcel. At last they found
the courtyard, hidden behind a wall of ivy. In its center sat a stone fountain
filled not with water, but with stars.

The new star floated down and became a kitten, bright as moonlight and twice
as curious. She had fallen from the Sky Archive, where constellations are
cataloged before they are hung above the world.

"Can you get me home?" she asked.

Professor Whisk studied the fountain. Juniper listened to the wind curling
through the ivy. Marmalade ate one emergency sardine and had an idea.

By sunrise, the cats had turned the observatory telescope toward the fountain,
polished the lens with a stolen silk handkerchief, and aimed a beam of dawn
straight into the stars. The kitten bounded up the light like a staircase,
pausing only to wave her tail.

That night, a new constellation appeared above the city: three heroic cats
standing on a roof, their tails pointed toward tomorrow.

The humans called it a weather anomaly. The cats updated the map.
